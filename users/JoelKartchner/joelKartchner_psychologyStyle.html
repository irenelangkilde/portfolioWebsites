About Me

I am a driven Electrical Engineering graduate student at Brigham Young University, currently pursuing a Master's degree under the advisement of Dr. Karl Warnick, with an anticipated graduation of April 2027. My undergraduate journey at BYU culminated in a B.S. in Electrical Engineering, completed Magna Cum Laude with a 3.98 GPA — a testament to my passion for the field and commitment to academic excellence. My research sits at the intersection of RF/microwave engineering, electromagnetic simulation, and precision fabrication, with a particular focus on high-frequency filter design and thin-film flexible interconnects.

Beyond the academic lab, I have gained hands-on industry experience through two summers at Raytheon's RF Products division in Tucson, Arizona, where I tested, simulated, and correlated real hardware behavior on complex RF circuit cards. I thrive in environments where theory meets physical implementation — whether I'm running electromagnetic field simulations in HFSS, writing test automation in Python, measuring S-parameters on a network analyzer, or developing a novel laser ablation process in the BYU clean room. I am also fluent in Portuguese, having served a two-year volunteer mission, which has given me a unique perspective on communication and cross-cultural collaboration.

My long-term goal is to contribute to the advancement of RF and microwave systems in defense, communications, or sensing applications — designing the next generation of compact, high-performance hardware that pushes the boundaries of what's physically possible. When I'm not in the lab, you'll find me mentoring students as a Teaching Assistant, organizing community events as a chapter officer, or chasing my curiosity with high-voltage hobby projects.

Selected Projects

80 GHz Radar Hardware Prototype

⭐ Senior Capstone Project · 2024–2025

80 GHz mmWave Radar System

High-velocity projectile detection in a controlled laboratory environment presents a uniquely demanding radar design challenge: the target is small, fast, and the system must discriminate it against background clutter with very high range and Doppler resolution. This Senior Capstone project tackled that challenge by designing and building a complete 80 GHz millimeter-wave (mmWave) radar system from the ground up.

Operating at 80 GHz provides extremely short wavelengths (~3.75 mm), enabling fine spatial resolution and compact antenna apertures. The system was designed to detect small, high-velocity projectiles — a requirement that demanded careful attention to signal bandwidth, pulse timing, and receiver dynamic range.

  • Full radar system design: waveform generation, RF front-end, signal processing chain
  • 80 GHz operating frequency for millimeter-level range resolution
  • Target: small, high-velocity projectile detection in a laboratory setting
  • Integrated PCB design, RF signal chain, and Python-based data acquisition and processing
  • Validated system performance against known target trajectories in lab conditions

Laser-Ablated RF Flex Cable under Microscope

BYU Research / Publication · 2023–2024

High-Density Flex Cables via Laser Ablation

Traditional PCB and flex cable manufacturing relies on chemical etching — a process constrained by minimum feature sizes and batch processing. Direct-write laser ablation offers a maskless, single-step alternative capable of patterning conductor traces at microscopic scales. This research project produced a functional tool and process for creating high-density RF-compatible flex cables with laser ablation, culminating in an IEEE publication.

  • Built and calibrated direct-write laser ablation system for thin-film patterning
  • Fabricated RF metal mesh filters and high-density flex cable interconnects
  • Characterized fabricated structures optically and with RF measurement tools
  • Published results at 2024 IEEE IETC Conference (see Publications section)

My Engineering Approach

Simulate First

Whether designing a filter, an RF exciter, or a fabrication process, I start with rigorous simulation — using HFSS, ADS, or Python models — before committing to hardware. Catching problems in simulation is fast and cheap; catching them on the bench is not.

Validate on Hardware

Simulation is only as good as its correlation to reality. I always verify simulated predictions with physical measurements, and I treat discrepancies as signals — they reveal modeling assumptions that need refinement or physical phenomena that weren't initially accounted for.

Document & Communicate

Good engineering work that isn't communicated clearly is only half-finished. I write clean code with documentation, produce well-structured lab reports, and have practiced distilling complex RF concepts into language that undergraduate students can understand and apply.
