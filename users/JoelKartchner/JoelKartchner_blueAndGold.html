About Me

I'm an Electrical Engineering graduate student at Brigham Young University, where I earned my BS Magna Cum Laude (GPA 3.98) and am now pursuing a Master's degree (current GPA 3.85) under the guidance of Professor Karl Warnick. My work sits at the exciting intersection of RF engineering, photonics, and laser-based fabrication — fields where precision at the micro and millimeter scale can directly shape the future of defense electronics, wireless communications, and sensing systems.

My research focuses on RF thin-film flex cables and microwave filter design, combining high-frequency electromagnetic simulation with hands-on hardware validation. Two summers at Raytheon in Tucson sharpened my industry instincts: I learned to bridge the gap between simulation and physical hardware, correlating SystemVue and ADS models against measurements from network analyzers and spectrum analyzers on real RF circuit cards destined for defense systems. Back at BYU, I led laser ablation research to fabricate microscopic metal-mesh RF filters and flexible cables — work that resulted in a peer-reviewed IEEE publication.

Beyond the lab, I'm passionate about mentoring. As a Teaching Assistant for Wireless Communication Circuits and EM Fields and Waves, I guide students through some of the most challenging topics in RF engineering. I hold the rank of Eagle Scout, served a two-year voluntary mission (where I became fluent in Portuguese), and have been actively involved in BYU's IEEE chapter. I thrive in environments that demand both deep theoretical understanding and practical engineering judgment.

My Engineering Approach

Whether designing a microwave filter, debugging an RF circuit card, or building a laser ablation tool, I follow a consistent methodology that connects theory to hardware outcomes.

1

Define the Problem

Start with specifications: frequency range, insertion loss, rejection, power handling, form factor. Ambiguous requirements are clarified before any simulation begins.

2

Analytic First Pass

Derive a closed-form estimate or prototype network — filter tables, transmission line theory, or equivalent circuit models — to establish a design starting point.

3

Simulate & Refine

Move into HFSS, ADS, or SystemVue for full-wave or circuit-level simulation. Sweep parameters systematically. Use Python to automate repetitive parameter studies.

4

Build & Measure

Fabricate the hardware — PCB, ablated structure, or assembled circuit card — then measure on the network or spectrum analyzer with calibrated setups.

5

Correlate & Iterate

Compare simulation to measurement. Understand discrepancies — are they modeling assumptions, material tolerances, or fixture effects? Iterate until the two converge.
